## Product premise

Word Odyssey is a child-directed language adventure in which learning happens by traveling through a living semantic forest.

Words and other language concepts are places in an explorable world. Relationships such as similar, opposite, translation, common phrase, and related idea become paths a child can choose. Each choice reveals more of the forest and leaves a visible trace of the journey.

> Language learning is navigation through a semantic forest.

The first product focuses on Chinese words and meaningful short phrases. Sentence patterns, explicit grammar concepts, broader knowledge, and other concept types are possible later extensions, not first-version commitments.

## Owner-decided Prototype 1 direction

Prototype 1 is for approximately 12-year-old learners whose stronger/native language is English, whose English reading level is approximately U.S. Grade 7, and whose Chinese proficiency is approximately Chinese elementary-school Grade 3. Chinese is the target language; English is support and scaffolding.

The experience is optimized only for iPad in landscape orientation. Simultaneous iPhone support is not required for Prototype 1.

Chinese words and short phrases are the primary explorable places in the forest. Pinyin and concise English meaning are available when useful, but should support attention to Chinese rather than visually replace it.

Prototype 1 uses Simplified Chinese. Personalization uses nicknames and preset avatars; real names and personal photos are outside Prototype 1. Future research may explore family-selected values or faith-informed story themes, but only through explicit family choice—never as an inferred or silent default.

## The child’s role

The child is:

- **Explorer:** chooses paths and discovers language neighborhoods.
- **Collector:** leaves a visible map of words, regions, and routes encountered.
- **Director:** chooses the anchors, characters, and surprises that guide the story.

The AI is an improvisational guide. It connects the child’s ideas with coherent, level-appropriate language, but does not take creative control.

## Product promises

### To the child

- I can choose what looks interesting.
- My choices change the forest and the story.
- I can see where I am, where I can go, and where I have been.
- The adventure belongs to me because I choose its important ingredients.
- New words are useful parts of a world, not isolated facts to memorize.

### To the parent

Word Odyssey should help a child build a mental network of language through repeated, meaningful encounters. Early value comes from recognizing word relationships, revisiting words through different paths, and using words in a self-directed story—not from claiming comprehensive curriculum coverage.

## Enduring experience principles

### The graph is a world, not a diagram

The experience should feel mysterious, playful, and alive. Motion, light, darkness, proximity, imagery, and sound should communicate meaning without requiring graph terminology.

### Reveal the forest progressively

The child begins with a small number of meaningful choices. The forest grows through action. Previously encountered places remain as personal history, while unrevealed vocabulary stays hidden during ordinary play.

### Make every move legible

The experience should answer, primarily through visuals:

1. Where am I now?
2. Where can I go next?
3. Where have I already been?

### Preserve creative agency

The child supplies important words, characters, names or nicknames, avatars, animals, objects, places, feelings, surprises, and decisions. The AI supplies connective language, humor, and gentle surprise. Story generation advances incrementally so the child can frequently redirect it.

### Let the story emerge from exploration

Story mode grows from the regions and paths the child explored. Child-selected anchors must matter. New story words become explorable parts of the same forest.

> The story lights up the forest.

### Keep exploration and story continuous

The defining loop is:

> Explore → Story → Pause and explore → Resume

Story is not a separate content feed, and exploration is not a detour from the adventure.

### Preserve the meaning of connections

Semantic relationships, the route the child traveled, and story-created narrative connections are different kinds of truth. The product must never present one as another.

### Prefer wonder over optimization

The first experience should prioritize beauty, clarity, surprise, humor, and voluntary return. Curriculum optimization, scoring, and adaptive review may deepen the product later.

## Intended emotional arc

1. **Invitation:** choose a word and world.
2. **Discovery:** reveal a small neighborhood.
3. **Agency:** choose paths and destinations.
4. **Ownership:** watch personal regions and routes accumulate.
5. **Composition:** choose story anchors from discoveries.
6. **Wonder:** see the dark forest light with story words.
7. **Direction:** pause, explore, add, and redirect.
8. **Continuation:** leave with a visible journey and a reason to return.

## What Word Odyssey is not

The first product is not:

- a chatbot with decorative nodes;
- a complete dictionary visualization;
- a linear course or constant quiz;
- an AI story generator that takes a prompt and finishes the adventure alone;
- a passive animated book;
- a rewards system centered on pressure, streaks, or ranking;
- a commitment to any technical architecture.

## Product success

The idea is promising if children can begin with little instruction, make meaningful semantic moves, recognize their own route, build more than one region, choose story anchors, see those anchors matter, interrupt a story to explore a new word, and deliberately influence what happens next.

The detailed first experience and observable success thresholds are defined in [First Playable Experience](first_playable_experience.md) and [Child Test Plan](child_test_plan.md).
