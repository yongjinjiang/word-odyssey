# Word Odyssey: Prototype Decisions

## Authority of this document

This is the product decision register for Prototype 1. It distinguishes owner-decided direction, enduring principles, provisional defaults, test-dependent questions, and intentionally deferred choices. When another document conflicts with a Prototype 1 default, this register controls until updated.

## Owner-decided Prototype 1 direction

These questions are resolved:

1. **Audience:** approximately 12 years old.
2. **Language profile:** English is native/stronger at approximately U.S. Grade 7 reading level; Chinese proficiency is approximately Chinese elementary-school Grade 3.
3. **Language direction:** English-supported learning of Chinese. Chinese is the target language; English is scaffolding.
4. **Device:** Prototype 1 is optimized only for iPad in landscape orientation. Simultaneous iPhone support is not required.
5. **Chinese writing system:** Prototype 1 uses Simplified Chinese.
6. **Identity boundary:** Prototype 1 uses nicknames and preset avatars. Real names and personal photos are not used.
7. **Values and themes:** future research may explore family-selected values or faith-informed story themes. Any such experience must require explicit family choice and must never infer or silently impose a worldview.

## Decided product principles

1. Language learning is navigation through a semantic forest.
2. The child is explorer, collector, and director.
3. The AI connects ideas but does not take creative control.
4. Explore → Story → Pause and explore → Resume is one continuous loop.
5. The forest is progressively revealed through child action.
6. Current, Available, Visited, Seen, and Unrevealed have distinct meanings.
7. Actual path history remains visible as the child’s journey.
8. Semantic, Traversed, and Narrative connections remain distinct.
9. Stories advance incrementally, usually one or two sentences at a time.
10. Story readiness is flexible; it is not permanently fixed at three regions.
11. Confirmed anchors matter meaningfully to the story.
12. Child identity and worldview are never silently inferred or imposed.

## Recommended defaults for Prototype 1

| Decision | Prototype 1 default | Why | Evidence that would overturn it |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary node language | Chinese characters are the identity of learning nodes | Keeps attention on the target language | Learners consistently require English to identify nodes before they can make meaningful choices |
| Pinyin | Visible as a secondary line in Adventure View; available on tap elsewhere | Supports independent reading without replacing characters | Learners ignore characters and navigate only by pinyin, or cannot decode nodes when pinyin is reduced |
| English support | Concise gloss on tap/first introduction; full sentence translation on demand | Makes English a scaffold rather than the experience’s main language | Comprehension collapses, or always-visible English demonstrably improves Chinese attention rather than replacing it |
| English as graph node | Not used in Prototype 1 | Keeps semantic traversal inside Chinese | Cross-language comparison proves essential to understanding or motivation |
| Starting choices | 4–6 Chinese words and 3 age-appropriate themes | Choice without overload | Repeated hesitation, random tapping, or requests for more variety |
| Available candidates | Usually 3–6; never more than 8 | Maintains readable Chinese labels and touch targets | Learners scan easily and ask for more, or remain overloaded |
| Relationship set | 近义词 Similar, 反义词 Opposite, 相关词 Related, 常用搭配 Common combinations | Linguistically useful at the target level | Labels are misunderstood or examples produce ambiguous membership |
| Relationship persistence | Reset after every focus move | Makes each turn intentional | Learners repeatedly reselect the same relationship with visible annoyance or expect continuous browsing |
| Reciprocal former node | Former Current becomes Visited inactive; returns as Available only after reselecting the reciprocal relationship | Preserves reset and history meaning | Learners cannot find their way back or misunderstand reciprocity |
| Seen entry from Map View | Preview allowed; direct entry not allowed | Preserves Seen versus Visited | Learners repeatedly expect direct entry and become frustrated without gaining route understanding |
| Returning to Visited | Direct return from Map View; no new Traversed edge | Supports orientation without inventing a relationship | Learners confuse return with semantic travel or use it to bypass exploration |
| Story-word tap | Tap automatically pauses and enters Adventure View; explicit Pause also exists | Makes curiosity immediate | Accidental transitions are common or learners think the story ended |
| Lit language nodes | Anchors, one useful reused Chinese word, and 1–2 central new Chinese words; max 4 at once | Supports recognition and controlled novelty | Learners cannot map sentence to stars, or important words feel unavailable |
| Function words | Do not light ordinary function words | Avoids noise | A later grammar-focused activity makes one instructionally meaningful |
| New story-word placement | Semantic home first; otherwise Narrative bridge; otherwise just beyond active region | Balances meaning, story motion, and mystery | Learners infer false Semantic relationships or cannot locate the word |
| Forest simplification | Preserve Current, Available, recent path, anchors, active story words, and region/path structure; cluster or mute distant history | Protects current action while retaining ownership | Learners lose recent words or mistake clusters for vocabulary |
| Story readiness | Two explored regions plus two anchors; optional preset-avatar character | Reaches story within a short test | Learners do not understand regions/anchors or story lacks ownership |
| Beat language | 1–2 short Chinese sentences appropriate to Grade 3 proficiency and 12-year-old interests | Keeps language accessible without feeling childish | Learners find prose too hard, too simple, or emotionally young |
| Beat novelty | 1–2 new explorable Chinese words; max 4 lit language nodes | Preserves comprehension and exploration rhythm | Recall or attention evidence supports another quantity |
| Time to first story | Approximately 4–6 minutes | Enough ownership in a 10-minute test | Learners lose interest before story or feel rushed into it |
| Whole forest | Current session’s revealed forest only | Keeps Map View meaningful and bounded | Longitudinal persistence becomes essential to the first test question |

## Unresolved questions requiring learner testing

### Chinese scaffolding

- Does always-visible pinyin support character learning or become the only text learners read?
- When should the concise English gloss appear: first reveal, tap, long press, or explicit support control?
- Does on-demand full-sentence English translation preserve productive struggle or create avoidable confusion?
- Can learners understand Chinese relationship labels with small English subtitles?
- Which relationship examples feel linguistically clear at Chinese Grade 3 proficiency?
- Should English translations later become true cross-language nodes, or remain annotations?

### Comprehension and control

- Can learners distinguish Current, Available, Visited, and Seen without a legend?
- Does resetting the relationship feel clear or repetitive?
- Does preview-only Seen behavior preserve exploration or frustrate?
- Is direct story-word auto-pause discovered and understood?
- Can learners distinguish Semantic, Traversed, and Narrative lines?

### Quantity, maturity, and rhythm

- Are 3–6 Chinese candidates exciting rather than overwhelming?
- Are two regions and two anchors understandable?
- Is 4–6 minutes the right time to first story?
- Are story themes and visual treatments engaging for 12-year-olds rather than childish?
- Are 1–2 new Chinese words per beat memorable without slowing the story?

## Product-owner decision status

The current owner-direction questions are resolved. Learner-testing questions above remain open, but they do not change the decided audience, language direction, device, writing system, identity boundary, or opt-in worldview rule.
